Research

Alzheimer's Disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia (60–70% of all cases), and affects 5% of the population over 60 years1. AD is pathologically characterized by massive neuronal loss, the presence of amyloid-beta (Aβ) plaques, and Tau neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) in the brain. Despite extensive studies of AD, there are still limited therapeutic options that can effectively treat this devastating disease. Identifying new molecular players in AD pathogenesis is important to offer further insights and potential targets for combating this disease. However, so far, only a few FDA-approved drugs–including Aducanumab, Lecanemab and Donanemab–have been approved for AD. Consequently, urgent effort is needed to demonstrate the molecular mechanisms of AD progression.

My long-term career goal is to uncover the molecular mechanisms underlying neurodegenerative diseases, particu-larly AD.

1. Decode LINE1’s Role in Genomic Instability and 3D Genome Disruption in Alzheimer’s Disease

Recent studies have shown that retrotransposons, such as Long Interspersed Nuclear Elements (LINE1 or L1), are activated in AD. Thus, the exact triggers for FL-L1 reactivation in AD are not fully understood. Biomarker Develop¬ment: Understanding the role of FL-L1 activation in AD could lead to the development of new biomarkers.

Targeting the pathways that lead to FL-L1 inactivation, or developing strategies to block its activity, could be a novel therapeutic approach. For example, pharmacological agents that enhance the epigenetic repression of FL-L1s might reduce genomic instability and slow the progression of AD.

2. Elucidate the Functions and Mechanisms of Silencers in Alzheimer’s Disease

Silencers are genetic regulatory elements that inhibit gene expression by recruiting repressive complexes and alter-ing chromatin structure. They function through mechanisms involving histone deacetylases (HDACs), DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs), and repressor proteins like REST. Silencers also engage in long-range chromatin interactions, bringing distant genomic regions closer to modulate the activity of promoters or enhancers.

In AD, characterized by dysregulation of genes related to synaptic function, inflammation, and cellular stress re-sponses, silencers may play a critical role in disease progression. They can aberrantly suppress neuroprotective genes or those involved in synaptic plasticity, exacerbating AD pathology. Identifying AD-specific silencers and their target genes is an emerging research area that offers new insights into the molecular mechanisms of AD.
